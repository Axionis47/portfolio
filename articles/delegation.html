Delegation is a Cost Function

Autonomy Calibration

I think of agent autonomy as four layers. Tools are pure functions with zero discretion. Operators make one narrow LLM judgment, like a classification or extraction. Planners chain multiple steps with bounded loops. Full agents run open-ended until a goal is met.

My instinct was always to reach for the highest layer. Let the agent figure it out. It felt powerful. But I kept finding that every degree of freedom I gave the model was a degree of unpredictability I then had to monitor, evaluate, and recover from.

A tool fails in one predictable way. A planner chains five decisions, each with its own failure mode, and the error surface multiplies. I learned to ask: what is the minimum autonomy that solves this step? And underneath that: can I afford the verification cost of the autonomy I am granting?

A tightly contracted operator can be verified cheaply with a schema check or a unit test. A loose agent requires human review, trajectory analysis, outcome monitoring. The cost of verification scales with the freedom granted. That tradeoff is something I did not appreciate early on.

Most of the tasks I worked on fit in the operator or planner layers. Simple tasks needed zero autonomy, just code. Fuzzy but checkable tasks used operators. I only reached for fully open-ended agents when the task genuinely demanded unpredictable multi-step reasoning.

I stopped handing out freedom I could not verify.
