Delegation is a Cost Function

Autonomy Calibration

There are four layers of autonomy in agentic systems. Tools execute deterministically — pure functions with zero discretion. Operators make one narrow LLM judgment — a classification, an extraction, a rewrite. Planners chain multiple steps with bounded loops and explicit stop conditions. Full agents run open-ended until a goal is met.

Most teams default to more autonomy than they need because it feels powerful. But every degree of freedom you hand the LLM is a degree of unpredictability you must now monitor, evaluate, and recover from.

A Level 0 tool fails in one predictable way. A Level 2 planner chains five decisions — each with its own failure mode — and the error surface multiplies. Fully autonomous agents bring higher cost and compounding error. They require extensive sandbox testing and strong guardrails.

The real question is never "should this be an agent?" It's: "what is the minimum autonomy that solves this step?" And underneath that: "can I afford the verification cost of the autonomy I'm granting?"

A tightly contracted operator can verify its output cheaply — a schema check, an exact match, a unit test. A loose agent requires expensive human review, trajectory analysis, outcome monitoring. The cost of verification scales with the freedom granted.

Autonomy calibration means ascending layers only as needed. Most tasks fit in the operator or planner layers. Simple tasks need zero autonomy — just code. Fuzzy-but-checkable tasks use Level 1-2. Fully open-ended autonomy is reserved for tasks that truly demand unpredictable multi-step reasoning — and even then, success depends on clear stop conditions, modest budgets, and rigorous monitoring.

The loose vs. tight tension is really about trust boundaries. How much can you afford to trust this component? The answer is always: only as much as you can afford to verify.

Don't hand out freedom you can't account for.
