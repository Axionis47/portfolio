Eval Defines the Trust Contract

You can only trust what you can measure. Different systems, different contracts.

Every system I have built ships with an implicit contract: "trust this output because we verified it this way." The evaluation is the contract. If you change the eval, you change what the system is allowed to claim. If you have no eval, you have no contract — you are shipping faith.

The contracts look completely different across projects, and they should. The GNN recommendation system's contract is Recall@10 — did the user interact with what we ranked highly? It is a behavioral metric. It does not care about the model's internal representations or attention patterns. It cares whether the recommendation was useful. 38.28% on 82K items. That number is the contract.

The knowledge distillation pipeline's contract is nDCG@10 relative to the teacher. 0.882 vs. 0.910. The contract is not "is the student good?" but "how much quality did we lose in compression?" A 3% gap is acceptable for 100x speed. A 15% gap would not be. The metric defines the acceptable trade-off.

The credit risk engine's contract is multi-dimensional. AUC for discrimination power. Calibration for regulatory compliance. Fairness ratios for legal compliance. A model with 0.95 AUC that fails the 4/5 rule is a lawsuit. The eval is not one number — it is a surface, and every point on that surface is a different contractual obligation.

LaneGuard's contract is mIoU plus conformal coverage guarantee. 0.806 mIoU says the segmentation is accurate. The conformal sets say the uncertainty is calibrated. Together they say: the model is right most of the time, and when it might be wrong, it tells you. Neither metric alone is sufficient.

The OSCE grader's contract is zero hallucinated assessments by design. Not "low hallucination rate." Zero. The citation-gating mechanism makes it structurally impossible to generate an unsupported assessment. The eval is a design invariant, not a measured probability.

The AI transformation agent's contract is coverage and evidence traceability. 89.7% coverage across 8 industries, with every claim traceable to a retrieved source. The contract does not claim the recommendations are correct — it claims they are grounded and comprehensive.

Each of these contracts is honest about what it guarantees and what it does not. The eval does not prove the system works. It defines the specific, bounded sense in which the system works.

Show me your eval, and I will tell you what your system actually promises.
