Compression Preserves What You Measure

When you shrink a model, only the knowledge you evaluate survives.

Every form of model compression is a bet. You are betting that the knowledge you care about will survive the squeeze and the knowledge you do not care about will be what gets dropped. The only way to win that bet is to measure exactly what matters.

The semantic search distillation made this explicit. I compressed a 560M-parameter cross-encoder into a 33M bi-encoder. The 3-component loss — MarginMSE, KL divergence, and contrastive — told the student exactly what to preserve: the teacher's ranking behavior, the teacher's uncertainty distribution, and hard-negative discrimination. The result was 97% of the teacher's nDCG at 100x the speed. But that 97% is only meaningful because nDCG measures the thing I actually needed: retrieval ranking quality. If I had measured accuracy instead, I might have gotten 99% while silently destroying the ranking.

LaneGuard's LoRA is compression in a different form. Instead of training all 3.7M parameters, I injected 131K trainable LoRA parameters across 32 attention layers — 3.4% of the model. The bet was that road segmentation knowledge could be learned in a low-rank subspace of the pretrained SegFormer's attention weights. It worked: 0.806 mIoU from 200 training images in 3 epochs on a CPU. But I only know it worked because mIoU measures pixel-level segmentation quality. The compression preserved what the metric tracked.

The GNN recommendation system taught me the most counterintuitive version. During ablation studies, I removed the FFN layers entirely and accuracy went up by 1.6% while parameters dropped 2.4x. The "knowledge" in those layers was not helping — it was noise that the model had memorized. Compression did not just preserve what mattered; it eliminated what was actively hurting.

The lesson applies beyond model weights. The causal inference agent's pull-based context engineering is compression of a different kind: 70% token savings by only feeding each agent the context it needs. The "knowledge" that got dropped was irrelevant context that was making decisions worse.

You cannot compress what you cannot measure. Define the metric first, then squeeze.
