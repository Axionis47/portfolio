Sirius Motorsports: Embedded BMS Firmware for a Lead-Acid EV Prototype

Engineering Brief
Role Battery Simulation Intern, 2022, Chennai, India Problem A 24V lead-acid battery pack (two 12V SLA in series) for a portable EV prototype (~20km range) needed embedded protection firmware. Without reliable cutoffs, deep discharge damages the batteries and overcurrent risks hardware failure. Approach Bare-metal C on an ARM Cortex-M MCU. ADC-based monitoring of pack voltage (resistive divider, 62k/10k), discharge current (shunt resistor + amplifier), and temperature (NTC thermistor). State machine with hysteresis-guarded thresholds. Software averaging to filter sensor noise. Result Zero false fault triggers in final validation. Undervoltage cutoff at 21.0V (1.75V/cell), overcurrent at 6A, thermal fault at 55C. All thresholds calibrated against bench instruments. Stack Embedded C, ARM Cortex-M (STM32), ADC, UART, NTC Thermistor, Shunt Resistor, Oscilloscope, Electronic Load

The firmware

Three ADC channels read pack voltage (resistive divider, 62k/10k), discharge current (shunt resistor + amplifier), and battery temperature (NTC thermistor). A 5-state machine (IDLE, CHARGING, DISCHARGING, WARNING, FAULT) enforces protection logic. The hysteresis gap between cutoff (21.0V) and reset (25.0V) is intentionally wide at 4V because the lead-acid pack rebounds 1.5-2V when the load disconnects. A textbook 0.5V gap caused relay chatter in early testing.

Most of the internship was calibration and debugging: integer math truncating the divider calculation (code cut off at 20.5V instead of 21.0V), ADC reference drift (3.28V instead of 3.30V), noisy shunt readings from load switching transients (fixed with software averaging), and battery sag under load triggering premature warnings. Each fix: modify C code, reflash MCU, re-run bench test.
