RU Health Hack 3: Citation-Gated OSCE Grader
The model is the only untrusted component in the system. Every judgment must cite one verbatim transcript line, and the citation is checked in code.
Engineering Brief
Event RU Health Hack 3, 9-person team, author as team lead, winner. Problem OSCE grading is slow, inconsistent across evaluators, and leaves no evidence trail when a student disputes a score. Approach A per-section, fully-cited design: segment the transcript once, then grade each section or teacher category against only its matched or full-transcript evidence. Every judgment is gated behind three layers, a prompt contract, constrained decoding plus field validation, and a code-level quote verification against the transcript. Result ~18 model calls per evaluation, down from ~150 in the original build. Every cited quote is verified against the transcript in code. Why it matters Wrong feedback shapes how a future doctor practices. Every failure in this system traces back to a human decision, not a model guess. Chapter 1. The LLM is not the doctor

An OSCE, an objective structured clinical exam, puts a medical student in a room with a standardized patient and scores the whole encounter: the history taken, the exam performed, the diagnosis reasoned through, the plan explained back to the patient. Grading it by hand is slow, and it drifts. Two evaluators watching the identical encounter disagree about whether a question got asked at all. Once the encounter ends, the record of what happened is an evaluator's memory and a page of handwritten notes. A student who disputes the score has nothing to point to, and neither does the evaluator.

The obvious fix is wrong. Point a large language model at the transcript and ask it to grade, and the model answers with everything it already believes about medicine, invited or not. Handed a gap in the history, it fills the gap with training-data medicine nobody in the room said. Handed an ambiguous answer, it reasons forward to a diagnosis the student never reached and grades as though the student reached it. The grade that comes back is confident. Confidence is not the same thing as evidence.

The separation we built instead: the LLM is the language expert, never the medical expert. A clinician writes the rubric, and the rubric carries every piece of clinical knowledge in the system: which questions matter, which diagnoses to expect, which can't-miss conditions have to surface somewhere in the encounter. The model answers one narrow question, over and over: does this piece of transcript text satisfy this one rubric criterion, and if so, cite the line. We treat the model as a component that will sometimes lie with total confidence, so the harness is built so a lie cannot become a grade. The quote it cites is checked against the transcript by code, not taken on faith.

Chapter 2. How much agency does the model get

Before a line of grading code existed, the team argued about how much clinical agency the model should get. Three options sat on the table. We rejected two of them.

Agency deliberation
OptionWhat the model decidesVerdict
Full agency Everything: what to ask, what is correct, what matters Rejected. Frozen knowledge, confident hallucination, wrong grades invisible until disputed.
Retrieval-grounded Grades from a medical-literature knowledge base Rejected. Months of work, and partial grounding is worse than none.
Zero clinical agency Nothing clinical: only whether transcript text satisfies a rubric criterion Chosen. Every failure traceable to a human decision.

The tradeoff sits underneath the chosen row. The system is only as good as the rubric a clinician writes: a vague item still grades badly even with a perfect harness wrapped around it. But that failure mode is visible and fixable. A teacher opens a YAML file, spots the gap, and edits it. A model quietly inventing clinical judgment fails in a way nobody can see until a student disputes a grade, and that is neither visible nor fixable in the same way. What actually makes a rubric good enough to grade against gets its own chapter.

Chapter 3. Three steps, eighteen calls

The pipeline is three steps, and only two of them touch the model at all. Step 1 is a single call: the transcript arrives with every line numbered in code, and the model's only job is to return line ranges for each of the 12 structural sections, nothing else. It never writes the segment text back; the harness reconstructs each segment from the line map the model returns, so a garbled word inside a range can't corrupt what the grader actually reads. Any section ID the model invents that isn't one of the 12 gets dropped, with a warning logged, not silently accepted. Step 2 is where the real work happens, and it happens all at once: one call per section, all of it under a single nested asyncio.gather. The structural sections and the teacher sections each gather internally, and the outer call gathers those two gathers, so eighteen calls in flight cost about the same wall-clock time as one. The 12 structural sections each see only their matched segment, and if a student circles back to a topic later in the encounter, every occurrence is aggregated together under its true starting line number, not just the first one found. The teacher sections, one per checklist category plus investigations, management, and a synthesized Diagnosis & Safety section, each see the full numbered transcript instead, because a case-specific behavior like ordering a stat ECG can surface anywhere in the encounter, not only in the section named for it. Step 3 touches the model exactly once more, for the narrative. Everything else in it is arithmetic.

A section with no matched segment never reaches the model at all. If Step 1 can't find History of Present Illness anywhere in the transcript, its items are marked not_satisfied in code, reasoning fixed to "Section not found in transcript," and that's the entire cost: no tokens, no latency, no chance of the model fishing for evidence in a segment that was never the right one. Retries only cover failures that are actually transient. A JSON parse failure or a dropped connection gets up to three attempts with exponential backoff, one second then two. A contract violation, a response missing a required grounding field, never gets a second try: it's a defect in that response, not a network hiccup, and retrying it would just spend tokens rediscovering the same defect.

I chose per-section calls over per-item calls because the original build made one call per rubric item, roughly 150 of them per evaluation, and batching a section's items into a single call cuts that to about 18 with no loss the schema can't catch: every item in a batched section still returns its own judgment, its own evidence_quote, its own reasoning, so nothing about per-item accountability is lost by sharing a prompt. asyncio.gather keeps wall-clock time flat under either design, so the drop from 150 to 18 is pure cost saved, not a latency tradeoff accepted.

I chose to let sections fail closed rather than search harder for them. A section the segmenter can't find gets marked not_satisfied without ever reaching the model, which is cheaper, but the real reason is honesty. A missing section is genuinely missing, and the harness would rather say so in code than send the model rummaging through the wrong segment looking for evidence that was never going to be there.

Chapter 4. The life of one section

The fastest way to understand the harness is to follow one section through it, from a raw line in the transcript to a number sitting in the final report.

01
The transcript gets line numbers
Every line is prefixed with its number in code, "14: When did the pain start?", before anything reaches a model. Citations later in the pipeline point back at these numbers, so the numbering has to exist before grading starts, not after.
02
Segmentation returns ranges, not text
One model call maps the numbered transcript to the 12 structural sections and returns only line ranges plus a list of unmatched lines, never the segment text itself. The ranges are enough: the harness rebuilds each segment from its own line map, so the citation for S3.1a will point at a line the model never rewrote.
03
One call grades a whole section
S3_HPI, History of Present Illness, carries 10 checklist sub-items, S3.1a through S3.1j. All 10 go to the model in a single call, and that call sees only the matched HPI segment, nothing else in the transcript. Teacher sections, the case's checklist categories, investigations, management, and a synthesized Diagnosis & Safety section, each get the full transcript instead, because case-specific behavior can turn up anywhere in the encounter. Every section call, structural and teacher alike, runs concurrently under asyncio.gather.
04
Every item comes back cited or empty
Each item in the response carries judgment, evidence_quote, evidence_location, and reasoning. The contract is fabrication-free: no evidence in the transcript means empty strings in both evidence fields and a not_satisfied judgment, never a guess dressed up as a finding.
05
The quote is checked, in code
_verify_citation() normalizes both the quote and the transcript and requires the quote to appear as a literal substring. A quote that fails is blanked and " [citation unverified]" is appended to the reasoning. The judgment itself never changes: verification only ever removes evidence, it does not re-grade.
06
Scores are arithmetic, not vibes
A section score is a weighted mean of its item judgments: satisfied counts 1.0, partial 0.5, not_satisfied 0.0. A code backstop, _coerce_judgment(), bars binary and critical items from keeping partial credit: even if a model hands one out, it's coerced to not_satisfied before scoring runs. The overall score is 0.4 × structural + 0.6 × teacher, both weights read from config.yaml, not decided at grading time.
07
The report holds every citation
The student sees each item's judgment badge next to the verified quote and its line number, or "no evidence cited" where none exists. Sections with no matched segment never reached the model at all: their items were marked not_satisfied in code, at zero model cost.

Chapter 5. The citation gate, three layers

Layer one is a prompt contract. Every grading call is told to cite exactly one verbatim quote, no paraphrase, plus the line it came from, "Line 14", and if nothing in the transcript supports the item, to leave evidence_quote and evidence_location as empty strings and return not_satisfied instead of inventing something to fill the gap. Layer two is structural. The response has to arrive as application/json with five required fields, item_id, judgment, evidence_quote, evidence_location, reasoning, and that schema is handed to Vertex AI's generation config for constrained decoding: a restriction applied at the token level, so the model cannot emit invalid JSON even if it wanted to. That still leaves room for a technically valid response missing one of the five, so the client independently re-checks every required field anyway, top level and inside each graded item, in _validate_grounding_fields(). A missing field is not a retry candidate. It is a contract violation, and the call aborts on the spot; only JSON-parse failures and connection errors get a second try, up to three attempts with exponential backoff, one second then two. Layer three is the part most systems skip. After grading finishes, the harness reopens the transcript and checks, in code, that each cited quote actually appears in it: _verify_citation() normalizes both sides first, lowercasing the text, stripping line-number and speaker prefixes like "Student:" and "Patient:", collapsing whitespace, and turning curly quotes into straight ones, then requires the quote to be a literal substring of what remains. A prompt can be ignored, and a schema can be satisfied by a fluent lie. Only reading the transcript back catches that.

Stack the three layers and look at what they do to incentives, not just to correctness. An empty evidence_quote paired with not_satisfied costs nothing: it is the honest answer when a transcript does not support an item, and it clears every layer without friction. A missing field costs the whole call: no retry, the request aborts, and the gap becomes a bug to fix in the harness, not a response to quietly accept. An invented quote gets further before it is caught. It satisfies the prompt's instructions on its face and the schema's required fields in full, and only breaks at layer three, where it is blanked and "[citation unverified]" is appended to the reasoning, the judgment itself left untouched. No judgment in this system ships without a verifiable citation or an explicit not_satisfied, and every quote that does ship is checked against the transcript in code, not assumed from the prompt. Honesty is cheap. Fabrication is expensive, and it gets more expensive the later it survives.

Three layers of enforcement
LayerMechanismWhat it stops
Prompt One verbatim quote plus its line number required per item; empty string and not_satisfied when nothing qualifies; never fabricate. Casual ungrounded judgments, the model asserting a verdict without pointing at text.
Schema Constrained decoding restricts output at the token level; _validate_grounding_fields() independently re-checks every required field; a violation aborts the call, no retry. Structurally ungrounded output reaching the pipeline, and quiet degradation into a response nobody checked.
Code Each cited quote is checked as a literal substring of the normalized transcript; a failed check blanks the quote and tags the reasoning, judgment left untouched. Fabricated or misattributed quotes surviving into a report a teacher reads as evidence.

The clip below runs four graded responses through all three layers at once. One arrives missing its quote and never reaches the grade book. One arrives honestly cited and clears both checks. One arrives confidently citing a line that says something else entirely, caught only after the fact. One arrives with nothing to cite and passes anyway, because saying so is the correct answer.

I chose to keep verification and judgment separate: the verifier can blank evidence but never flip a judgment, because a grading pass and an evidence audit are different jobs, and mixing them would let a string-matching bug silently change a student's score. The cost is stated plainly: a right judgment with a wrong citation keeps its points and loses its proof, and the flag makes that visible to the teacher.

Chapter 6. The rubric contract

The rubric is the single source of clinical truth in this system. The model never decides what counts as good history-taking, or which diagnosis is too dangerous to miss; a clinician decides that once, in a YAML file, and every evaluation afterward draws on the same decision. Rubric quality is therefore the ceiling on grading quality: a flawless harness scoring a vague rubric still returns a vague grade, just with a citation attached to the vagueness.

Two rubrics exist, and they carry different kinds of truth. A universal structural rubric, owned by the program rather than any single case, defines 12 sections and 49 gradeable items: the shape every OSCE encounter is expected to follow, from introduction and rapport through closure. A per-case teacher rubric carries everything specific to one patient scenario, and its contract is exactly 7 required blocks: case_name, case_description, checklist, diagnosis, investigations, management, reasoning_links.

01
Checkable from text, not vibes
The authoring guide runs a vague-vs-checkable table for exactly this reason: "Adequately explores gynecological history" becomes "Asks the date of the last menstrual period." If a clinician cannot point at the line that would satisfy an item, neither can the model.
02
Weight and critical, on every item
Every item declares a weight and a critical flag. Critical items get no partial credit and no benefit of the doubt: ambiguous evidence resolves to not_satisfied, by design, not by accident.
03
Can't-miss diagnoses default to critical
A can't-miss diagnosis is critical by default, even if the author forgets the flag. Each one becomes its own binary item in the synthesized Diagnosis & Safety section, judged against the full transcript: considered or not, with no partial credit available.
04
Uploads are validated on arrival
Rubrics are YAML, and they're validated the moment they arrive: a bad first upload bounces with a 400, a bad update with a 422, neither grades silently. An update validates against a temporary file before an atomic replace, and an identical re-upload dedupes by SHA-256 content hash instead of creating a duplicate record.
05
A checklist written for clinicians
The authoring guide closes with a 10-item pre-flight checklist, written for the clinicians who own the rubric, not the engineers who own the pipeline.

The honest boundary: the structural rubric also defines a reasoning chain, R1 through R4, and three 1-5 global dimensions, G1 through G3, that the restructured grader parses but does not yet score. They sit on the open list, not silently pretended away.

Chapter 7. What the harness buys, and what it costs

Every guarantee has a bill; the ledger, stated plainly.

Mechanism ledger
MechanismWhat it buysWhat it costs
Per-section batched calls ~18 calls per evaluation instead of ~150. One parse failure loses one section, not one item. A weak model could lose focus partway through a section; the schema and per-item validation are what make batching safe.
Citation gate, three layers No judgment ships without a verifiable citation or an explicit not_satisfied; disputes resolve by reading the cited line. More tokens per call, and a hard failure mode: a grounding violation aborts the evaluation rather than degrading silently.
Code-level quote verification A fabricated quote cannot survive into a report. Verification never flips judgments, so a right judgment with a wrong citation keeps its score and loses only its evidence.
Rubric as single source of clinical truth Failure is visible and fixable by a clinician editing YAML. Authoring is the bottleneck; a bad rubric grades badly at scale.
Synthesis firewall The narrative never sees the transcript, so it cannot cite evidence that was never graded. Narrative quality is capped by what item grading surfaced.

These costs were paid knowingly. When auditability and elegance pulled apart, I chose auditability.

Chapter 8. The version that won was heavier

The build that won the hackathon graded every rubric item with its own model call, then layered more on top: second-opinion dual grading, score bands, a review queue, reasoning-chain graph extraction, and global 1-5 ratings. Roughly 150 model calls per evaluation.

The restructure removed all of it from the grading path and kept the guarantees. One call per section, structural and teacher alike. Every item is still individually judged and individually cited, exactly as before. What changed is where the citation gets checked: not by a second model call marking its own homework, but by _verify_citation() in code, a plain substring match against the transcript. Eighteen calls per evaluation, down from roughly 150. Complexity was not what made the grades trustworthy; the citations were.

Two pieces of the old build still sit in the repo, unused since the restructure: an LCS-based (longest common subsequence, a string-matching algorithm) section-order analyzer, and the review queue's database table. The analyzer never touched a model, order is a deterministic string problem, so if it returns it will return as a cheap pure-code order score, not another call.

Chapter 9. What is proven, what is open

What exists today is tested where it counts, not everywhere. Thirty-two mocked tests cover the pipeline end to end: section segmentation, scoring arithmetic, judgment coercion on binary and critical items, citation verification against the transcript, auth scoping, and rubric upload hygiene. All of it runs against a FakeLLMClient, zero network calls, so the suite checks the harness logic, not the model's mood that day.

Listing what is not yet built is the boundary of what is done, not an apology.

01
Golden-transcript regression harness
Thirty-two sample transcripts, five students across five cases plus quality variants, exist for manual QA today. None of them are wired into an automated grading regression yet, so a prompt change is checked by eye, not by a diff against known-good grades.
02
Score or remove the unscored dimensions
The structural rubric still carries a reasoning chain (R1-R4) and global dimensions (G1-G3, 1-5 Likert). Both are parsed out of the YAML and neither is scored after the restructure. An honest open item: decide whether they earn a scoring path back in or come out of the schema entirely.
03
Inter-rater reliability study
Grade the same transcripts with the system and with human evaluators, then measure where the two agree and where they don't. No claim about grading quality holds without this comparison.
04
Real authentication
Current auth is synthetic and demo-only, flagged as such in the code. It has no place in front of a real classroom.
05
PostgreSQL for concurrent classrooms
SQLite serves the demo fine. It is not the answer for multiple classrooms running evaluations against the same database at once.
06
Streaming results
Segmentation first, then sections as they land, instead of one wait for the full pipeline to finish before anything renders.
07
LLM-assisted rubric authoring
The model drafts rubric items from a case description, the clinician judges. Authoring, not grading, is the actual bottleneck in scaling this system, and the model stays in the same role it holds everywhere else on this page: it drafts language, it does not decide what counts as good medicine.
