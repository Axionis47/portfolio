LaneGuard: Drivable Area Segmentation with Calibrated Uncertainty

Engineering Brief
Problem A self-driving car needs to know where it can drive. Neural networks give confident answers even when they are wrong. In safety-critical systems, that kills people. Approach Fine-tune SegFormer-B0 on BDD100K using manual LoRA injection (13.7% trainable params). Wrap predictions in conformal prediction sets with mathematical coverage guarantees. Project to Bird’s Eye View via IPM. Result 0.806 Mean IoU from training only 3.4% of parameters. 2.1× improvement over frozen baseline. 145 tests, full CI/CD, Vertex AI deployment. Why it matters You cannot deploy perception models that are confidently wrong. Conformal prediction gives you a guarantee: the true class is in the prediction set with probability ≥ 1−α. No neural network alone can promise that. Stack PyTorch, HuggingFace Transformers, OpenCV, GCP Vertex AI, Docker, GitHub Actions, W&B
Chapter 1. The problem no one talks about

Here is what a self-driving car actually needs to know, every single frame, 30 times per second: where can I drive? Not "what objects are around me." Not "is that a pedestrian or a mailbox." The most fundamental question is simpler: which pixels in this camera image represent road I can safely put my tires on?

This is called drivable area segmentation. You take a 1280×720 image from a dashboard camera, and you produce a binary mask: every pixel is either "drivable" or "not drivable." Road surface = 1. Sidewalk, grass, buildings, sky, other cars = 0.

Modern neural networks are good at this. SegFormer, DeepLab, U-Net — they all produce sharp masks with high accuracy. But there is a problem that the accuracy number hides. When the model says a pixel is drivable with 97% confidence, is it actually 97% likely to be correct?

No. Softmax outputs are not probabilities. They are normalized scores. A model can output 0.99 confidence on a pixel that is dead wrong — a shadow on the road, a puddle reflecting sky, a construction zone it has never seen before. In a spreadsheet, this is a rounding error. In a car moving at 60 mph, this is a steering decision into a ditch.

The question is not "how accurate is the model." The question is: when the model does not know, does it say so?

Chapter 2. Training 3.4% of the parameters

SegFormer-B0 has 3.8 million parameters, pretrained on Cityscapes — a dataset of urban street scenes with 19 semantic classes. It already understands roads, sidewalks, buildings, cars. You do not need to re-teach it what a road looks like. You need to adapt it: from 19-class Cityscapes segmentation to binary drivable-area segmentation on BDD100K.

Full fine-tuning would update all 3.8M parameters. But most of those parameters encode general visual knowledge that transfers directly. The encoder already extracts road-relevant features. What needs to change is the task-specific behavior — the attention patterns that route those features toward a binary decision.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) freezes the entire pretrained model and injects small trainable matrices into the attention layers. Instead of updating a weight matrix W directly, you learn two small matrices A and B such that the effective weight becomes W + BA. The rank of BA is much lower than W — rank 16 instead of the full dimension. This means you are learning a low-dimensional correction to the pretrained behavior.

The catch: HuggingFace PEFT does not natively support SegFormer’s architecture. So I wrote the LoRA injection from scratch. Every Query, Key, Value, and Dense projection in every attention block gets a parallel low-rank path. The math:

Chapter 3. BDD100K — what the camera sees

BDD100K is Berkeley’s large-scale driving dataset: 100,000 images from dashboard cameras across diverse conditions — day, night, rain, fog, highways, city streets. For drivable area segmentation, each image comes with a pixel-level semantic mask with 18 classes plus an ignore label.

I collapse this to binary: train_id 0 (road) = drivable, everything else = not drivable. The class balance is roughly 23% drivable to 77% non-drivable. This makes sense — most of any driving image is sky, buildings, trees, and other cars. The road is a relatively small strip at the bottom.

Training uses 7,000 images, validation uses 1,000. Images are resized from 1280×720 to 512×512 with ImageNet normalization. The only augmentation is horizontal flip at 50% probability. No color jitter, no random crop. The pretrained encoder expects clean, well-framed images — heavy augmentation would fight the pretraining.

Chapter 4. Does LoRA actually help?

Before training anything, I ran a controlled experiment. Take the pretrained SegFormer-B0 with a randomly initialized binary decode head. Run it on 100 validation images. Measure Mean IoU. This is the baseline: what you get from transfer learning with zero task-specific training.

Then inject LoRA adapters and train for just 3 epochs on 200 images at 256×256 resolution. On a CPU. In under 5 minutes. Measure again.

The baseline Mean IoU of 0.380 is not surprising. The pretrained decode head expects 19-class output. We replaced it with a randomly initialized 2-class head. The encoder features are excellent, but the head has no idea what to do with them. LoRA does not touch the head — it adapts the encoder’s attention patterns to route road-relevant features more effectively toward the new head. Three epochs is enough for the adapters to learn the mapping.

The verdict: unambiguously worth it. Not a marginal improvement. A 2.1× jump from training 3.4% of parameters for under 5 minutes on a CPU.

Chapter 5. The uncertainty problem

Now the model produces sharp binary masks with 0.806 Mean IoU. Good. But here is the question that IoU does not answer: on which pixels is the model guessing?

A softmax score of 0.95 does not mean "95% chance this is correct." It means "the model’s internal logit for this class was much larger than the other." A model trained on sunny California highways will output 0.99 confidence on a snow-covered road in New Jersey — because it has never seen snow, so it has no basis for doubt. The score is high precisely because the model lacks the experience to be uncertain.

Conformal prediction solves this with a mathematical guarantee. Instead of outputting a single class per pixel, you output a prediction set — a set of classes that is guaranteed to contain the true class with probability ≥ 1−α. If you set α = 0.1, the true class will be in the set at least 90% of the time. Not approximately. Not on average. Provably.

The mechanism is simple. You take a calibration set (held-out data the model has not seen). For each pixel, compute a non-conformity score: 1 minus the model’s softmax probability for the true class. High score means the model was uncertain about the correct answer. Then take the (1−α) quantile of all these scores, with a finite-sample correction. This gives you a threshold q̂.

At inference time, for each pixel, include every class whose softmax probability is ≥ 1−q̂. If both classes make the cut, the prediction set has size 2 — which means the model does not know. If only one class makes the cut, the model is confident. The set size is the uncertainty map.

Chapter 6. From camera view to bird’s eye view

A segmentation mask in camera view is useful for visualization. But a path planner does not think in pixels. It thinks in meters. "Is there drivable road 10 meters ahead and 2 meters to the left?" To answer that, you need to transform the mask from the camera’s perspective projection into a top-down Bird’s Eye View (BEV).

I use Inverse Perspective Mapping (IPM) — a classical homography-based approach. Under the flat-ground assumption (z = 0), you can compute a 3×3 matrix that maps pixels in the camera image to positions in a metric ground plane. No neural network needed. Pure geometry.

The BEV output is a 200×200 grid covering −10m to +10m laterally and 3m to 50m forward. Each cell tells you: drivable or not. An orange marker at the bottom represents the ego vehicle. This is directly consumable by a path planner — it is a top-down occupancy map in real-world coordinates.

Chapter 7. Production-grade engineering

This is not a notebook. It is a deployable system with 145 tests across 11 test modules, a CI/CD pipeline that gates every merge, Docker containerization, and cloud training on GCP Vertex AI.

Mixed precision (AMP) on CUDA, gradient clipping at max_norm=1.0, NaN/Inf detection with bad batch skipping, early stopping with patience=5, full checkpoint resume (model + optimizer + scaler + scheduler + epoch + metrics).
04
Vertex AI deployment
Dockerized training on n1-standard-8 + NVIDIA T4 (16GB VRAM). GCS-backed artifact storage. W&B experiment tracking. One command to submit a training job.
