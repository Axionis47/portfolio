Credit Risk Engine: Basel III ECL with Fairness Guarantees

Engineering Brief
Problem Approve or decline a loan application in real time. Get it wrong one way and you lose money. Get it wrong the other way and you violate federal law. Chapter 1. Three things a credit model must do at once

A credit risk model has three jobs, and failing any one of them is catastrophic.

Job 1: Predict accurately. If the model says a borrower has a 5% chance of default and they actually default 20% of the time, the bank loses money. The model must separate good borrowers from bad ones. This is measured by AUC and KS statistic.

Job 2: Explain every decline. Under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), if you deny a loan, you must tell the applicant why. Not "the model said no." Specific, actionable reasons: "high credit utilization," "insufficient payment history," "recent delinquency." A black-box model that cannot produce these reasons is not deployable, no matter how accurate.

Job 3: Do not discriminate. If the model approves 80% of one demographic group and 50% of another, you have a disparate impact problem. The 4/5 rule (EEOC guideline) says the approval rate for any protected group must be at least 80% of the majority group’s rate. A model that violates this is a lawsuit.

Most ML projects optimize for job 1 and ignore jobs 2 and 3. This project treats all three as hard constraints.

Each component is a separate model with its own training data and evaluation metrics.

PD is the headline model — a weighted ensemble of Logistic Regression, XGBoost, and LightGBM, with weights optimized via Nelder-Mead on validation AUC. XGBoost dominates at 98.59% weight because it has the highest discriminative power. The logistic model contributes stability. A Wide & Deep neural network (TensorFlow) is built and calibrated but disabled in production by config — available for benchmarking and future promotion.

LGD is a two-stage model. Stage one: a logistic classifier predicts whether any loss will occur at all (many defaults recover fully). Stage two: an XGBoost regressor predicts the severity of loss conditional on there being one. The final LGD is the product of both, clipped to [0, 1].

EAD models how much the borrower will owe at the moment of default. A credit card with a $10,000 limit and $3,000 drawn balance might be at $7,000 by the time default occurs. The Credit Conversion Factor (CCF) model predicts this utilization increase. Regulatory fallback: CCF = 0.75 when the model is unavailable.

Chapter 3. Sixty-three features from twenty inputs

The raw application has 20 fields: age, income, employment length, credit limit, utilization, delinquencies, and so on. These are necessary but not sufficient. The model needs behavioral signals — how the borrower actually uses credit over time, not just what their application says.

The feature engineering pipeline derives 63 features across 8 groups: demographic (age, log income, profile completeness), credit (utilization, DTI, delinquencies), velocity (transaction counts over 7/30/90 day windows), aggregation (spending by category, merchant diversity, entropy), behavioral (mobile %, weekend %, nighttime transactions), time series (spend trends over 3 and 6 months, volatility), payment (on-time rate, consecutive on-time streak, payment trend), and risk ratios (loan-to-income, balance-to-income, utilization × DTI interaction).

Training data is enriched with 6.4 million synthetic transactions and 720,000 payment records generated from Gaussian copula borrower profiles. The critical design decision: behavioral patterns (spending velocity, payment timeliness) are driven by observable credit features (DTI, utilization), not the default label. This prevents target leakage while creating realistic behavioral variation.

Chapter 4. Twenty reasons you might be declined

When the model declines an application (credit score below 550), it must produce up to four adverse action reason codes explaining why. Not vague descriptions. Specific, regulatory-compliant codes that map to features the applicant can understand and potentially improve.

The system computes SHAP values for every feature in the declined application. It filters to risk-increasing features only (SHAP value pushes probability of default higher). It excludes protected attributes (age, state) to prevent discrimination from appearing in the explanation. It ranks by absolute SHAP impact and returns the top four.

Chapter 5. The 4/5 rule — continuous auditing

A model can be accurate and still be illegal. If it approves 80% of one group and 55% of another, the disparate impact ratio is 55/80 = 0.69, which is below the 0.80 threshold (the "4/5 rule"). This is actionable regardless of intent.

The BiasMonitor tracks three fairness metrics continuously: demographic parity (approval rates within 10% across groups), equalized odds (true positive and false positive rates within 10%), and disparate impact ratio (must stay above 0.80). It stores a timestamped fairness history and alerts on degradation. This is not a one-time audit. It runs on every batch of predictions.

Chapter 6. Champion vs challenger — every request

The production model is the ensemble (champion). But you never know if a simpler model would do just as well — or if a new model would do better — unless you test it on live traffic. Shadow mode scores every incoming request through both the champion and a challenger (standalone XGBoost) in parallel. The champion drives the actual decision. The challenger runs silently.

The system tracks three metrics between champion and challenger: agreement rate (same decision on the same application — target ≥95%), PD correlation (are the probabilities calibrated similarly — target ≥0.98), and latency comparison. When the challenger meets all thresholds over a sufficient sample, the system flags it as "safe to promote." This is data-driven model promotion, not gut feel.

Chapter 7. Drift, degradation, and the retraining trigger

A model trained on 2024 data will decay as the economy changes. The Population Stability Index (PSI) measures how much each feature’s distribution has shifted since training. Three alert levels: stable (PSI < 0.10), warning (0.10–0.25), and alert (PSI ≥ 0.25, triggers retraining).

This aligns with regulatory expectations. Regulators do not just want a model that was validated once. They want evidence of ongoing monitoring — proof that you are watching for degradation and acting before the model starts making bad decisions. PSI per feature, AUC tracking over time, and fairness degradation alerts give you that audit trail.
