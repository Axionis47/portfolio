Project

Credit Risk Engine: Basel III ECL with Fairness Guarantees

Engineering Brief
Problem Approve or decline a loan application in real time. Get it wrong one way and the bank loses money. Get it wrong the other way and you violate federal law. The model needs to predict default probability, estimate dollar losses, explain every decline with specific reasons, and prove it is not discriminating. Approach 6-model system: PD ensemble (Logistic + XGBoost + LightGBM, Nelder-Mead weights), two-stage LGD, EAD with regulatory fallback, fraud scorer. 44 registered features across 7 groups (63+ after encoding). SHAP-based ECOA adverse action codes. Three fairness metrics tracked continuously. Shadow mode A/B testing. Result LightGBM 0.9418 AUC (standalone best), XGBoost 0.8729, Ensemble 0.8723, Logistic 0.8474. 20 adverse action codes. Demographic parity, equalized odds, and disparate impact ratio monitored per batch. Full Docker stack with PostgreSQL 16, Redis 7, MLflow, Prometheus. Why it matters A credit model that cannot explain its decisions is illegal under ECOA. A model that discriminates is illegal under the Fair Housing Act. Accuracy alone is not enough. This project treats accuracy, explainability, and fairness as simultaneous hard constraints. Stack XGBoost, LightGBM, scikit-learn, SHAP, Optuna, FastAPI, PostgreSQL, Redis, MLflow, Prometheus, Docker, GitHub Actions
Chapter 1. Three things a credit model must do at once

A credit risk model has three jobs, and failing any one of them is catastrophic.

Job 1: Predict accurately. If the model says a borrower has a 5% chance of default and they actually default 20% of the time, the bank loses money. The model needs to separate good borrowers from bad ones. This is measured by AUC and KS statistic.

Job 2: Explain every decline. Under the Equal Credit Opportunity Act (ECOA), if you deny a loan, you must tell the applicant why. Not "the model said no." Specific, actionable reasons: "high credit utilization," "insufficient payment history," "recent delinquency." A black-box model that cannot produce these reasons is not deployable, no matter how accurate.

Job 3: Do not discriminate. If the model approves 80% of one demographic group and 50% of another, you have a disparate impact problem. The 4/5 rule (EEOC guideline) says the approval rate for any protected group must be at least 80% of the majority group's rate. A model that fails this test has a regulatory problem.

Most ML projects optimize for job 1 and ignore jobs 2 and 3. I built this project to treat all three as hard constraints.

An application comes in as a JSON payload. FastAPI validates the schema, the feature engine computes 44 features from borrower, transaction, and payment data (with Redis caching for repeated lookups), and the model ensemble runs PD, LGD, EAD, and fraud scoring in sequence. On a decline, SHAP generates local feature contributions, the adverse action module maps them to ECOA-compliant reason codes, and the response goes back with a credit score, a decision, and up to four specific reasons.

The monitoring layer is not bolted on after the fact. Shadow mode scores a challenger model on every request in parallel with the champion. The fairness monitor checks three metrics on every batch. The drift detector computes PSI per feature against training distributions. All of this feeds into Prometheus, and all scoring results are logged to PostgreSQL for audit.

I chose a 6-model system over a single model because regulatory credit scoring requires separable components. PD, LGD, and EAD must be independently auditable for Basel III compliance. A single end-to-end model that outputs expected loss directly cannot explain which component drove the decision. Separating them also means each can be retrained independently when its specific domain drifts.

The raw application has about 20 fields. That is necessary but not sufficient. The feature engineering pipeline derives 44 registered features across 7 groups, expanding to 63+ after one-hot encoding of 4 categorical fields and target encoding of state.

Training data is enriched with 6.4 million synthetic transactions and 720,000 payment records generated from Gaussian copula borrower profiles. The critical design decision: behavioral patterns (spending velocity, payment timeliness) are driven by observable credit features like DTI and utilization, not the default label. This prevents target leakage while creating realistic behavioral variation.

For missing values, most nulls get median imputation. The exception is months_since_last_delinquency, which gets set to -1 when there is no delinquency history, with a separate binary has_delinquency flag. The absence of a delinquency is itself a signal, not missing data.

I chose 7 feature groups with a registry rather than raw feature engineering because feature provenance matters in regulated environments. Every feature traces to a group (demographic, credit history, velocity, aggregation, behavioral, time series, payment risk), and the registry documents what each computes and why it exists. When an auditor asks "why does this feature affect the score," the answer is in the registry, not in someone's head.

The PD ensemble is the headline model. Nelder-Mead optimizes the blend of Logistic Regression, XGBoost, and LightGBM on validation AUC. In practice, XGBoost dominates at 98.6% weight, with Logistic contributing 1.4% and LightGBM getting essentially zero. The ensemble is functionally a single XGBoost model with a thin logistic adjustment. This is a known limitation that I address in Chapter 7.

The LGD model is two-stage because many defaults recover fully. Stage one (logistic classifier) predicts whether any loss occurs at all. Stage two (XGBoost regressor) predicts the severity conditional on there being a loss. The final LGD is the product of both, clipped to [0, 1].

The EAD model predicts how much the borrower will owe at the moment of default. A credit card with a $10,000 limit and $3,000 drawn might be at $7,000 by default. The Credit Conversion Factor model predicts this utilization increase, with a regulatory fallback of CCF = 0.75 when the model is unavailable.

I chose Nelder-Mead for ensemble weight optimization because it is gradient-free and works directly on the AUC objective. Grid search over 3 model weights is feasible but coarse. Nelder-Mead finds the optimal weighting without requiring a differentiable loss. The result (XGBoost 98.6%, Logistic 1.4%, LightGBM ~0%) is informative even when it looks surprising: it tells you the ensemble collapsed, which is itself a diagnostic signal I discuss in Chapter 7.

Chapter 5. The model is not allowed to discriminate
01
Protected feature exclusion
Age and state_encoded are in the PROTECTED_FEATURES set. They are never surfaced in adverse action reasons, even if SHAP shows them as risk-increasing. The model may use them internally, but the explanation layer filters them out.
02
Three fairness metrics
Demographic parity: approval rates within 10% across groups. Equalized odds: true positive and false positive rates within 10%. Disparate impact ratio: must stay above 0.80 (the 4/5 rule). All three run on every batch of predictions via BiasMonitor.
03
Continuous monitoring
BiasMonitor stores a timestamped fairness history. check_degradation() alerts when any metric fails its threshold. This is not a one-time audit. It runs on every batch and feeds into Prometheus for alerting.

When the model declines an application (credit score below 550), the system computes SHAP values for every feature, filters to risk-increasing contributions only, excludes age and state from the candidate set, ranks by absolute SHAP impact, and returns the top four as ECOA-compliant adverse action codes. The applicant gets specific, actionable reasons, not "the model said no."

I chose three fairness metrics rather than one because demographic parity alone misses error rate differences across groups. Equalized odds captures whether the model makes the same types of mistakes regardless of demographics. Disparate impact ratio implements the FTC's 4/5 rule (approval rate of any group must be at least 80% of the majority). A model can pass one metric and fail another, so monitoring all three simultaneously is the only defensible approach.

I chose SHAP-based adverse action codes over rule-based reasons because SHAP reflects what the model actually uses, not what a human guesses it uses. The top risk-increasing features map to 20 ECOA-compliant codes, with protected features (age, state_encoded) explicitly excluded from the explanation set regardless of their SHAP magnitude.

Chapter 6. Shipping it
01
FastAPI scoring server
Async endpoints, Pydantic request/response validation, API key auth, rate limiting at 100 requests per minute, structured JSON logging via structlog. Models loaded on startup via lifespan hooks.
02
Docker Compose: 5 services
API (FastAPI on port 8000), PostgreSQL 16 (scoring results, drift metrics, fairness metrics), Redis 7 (feature caching, TTL 3600s), MLflow (experiment tracking, model registry), Prometheus (request counts, latency histograms, AUC, drift PSI, fairness disparity).
03
Shadow mode A/B testing
Champion and challenger scored in parallel on every request. Comparison report tracks agreement rate (target ≥95%), PD correlation (target ≥0.98), latency delta, and disagreement breakdown. When the challenger meets all thresholds, the system flags it as safe to promote.
04
PSI drift detection
Population Stability Index computed per feature against training distributions using 10-bin histograms. Three alert tiers: stable (PSI < 0.10), warning (0.10 to 0.25), alert (PSI ≥ 0.25, triggers retraining investigation).
05
CI/CD via GitHub Actions
Every push: ruff lint, mypy typecheck, 13 test files (unit + integration + performance). Weekly training pipeline (Sunday 06:00 UTC): synthetic borrower generation, full Optuna tuning, artifact upload with 30-day retention.

PSI is computed per feature, not globally. A single drifting feature triggers investigation before the whole model degrades.

I chose shadow mode A/B testing over direct model replacement because a challenger model that looks good on holdout data can behave differently in production. The shadow router scores both champion and challenger on every request, logs agreement rates, and only recommends promotion at 95%+ agreement with 0.98+ PD correlation. Below 80% agreement, the system returns "DO NOT PROMOTE" regardless of the challenger's offline metrics.

Chapter 7. What I would change

Every project has gaps. Listing them is not self-deprecation. It is proof that I understand the system well enough to know where it breaks.

01
The ensemble is not really an ensemble
XGBoost takes 98.6% of the weight after Nelder-Mead optimization. Logistic gets 1.4%. LightGBM gets essentially zero. Meanwhile, standalone LightGBM has an AUC of 0.9418, substantially higher than the ensemble's 0.8723. The optimizer found a local minimum. I would investigate why LightGBM dominates individually but gets zero ensemble weight, and whether the validation set used for weight optimization is representative. The fix is likely feature subsetting to force model diversity, or replacing Nelder-Mead with a constrained optimizer that enforces minimum weights.
02
Some velocity features may be near-zero variance
The velocity group has 10 features computed from synthetic transaction data. With a fixed reference date (2024-12-31), features like txn_count_7d and decline_rate_30d may have very low variance for many borrowers, especially on sparse real-world data. I would add a variance threshold gate in the feature pipeline to drop features that contribute noise rather than signal.
03
No fairness gate in the training pipeline
The system monitors fairness continuously in production via BiasMonitor, but the training pipeline does not include a fairness gate. Step 12 of the 14-step pipeline gates on AUC ≥ 0.70, but there is no check for disparate impact or demographic parity before the model is saved. A model could pass the performance gate and fail the fairness gate, and the pipeline would still save it. I would add a fairness gate at step 12.5.
04
Synthetic data is a double-edged sword
The behavioral features are computed from synthetic transactions generated via Gaussian copula. The copula preserves correlations from the seed profiles, but the distributions are smoother than real transaction data. Real-world data has heavy tails, seasonal patterns, and messy missing values that the synthetic generator does not fully capture. The model may be overfit to the synthetic distribution.
05
Shadow mode storage is ephemeral
ShadowResult objects are stored in an in-memory list. If the server restarts, all comparison data is lost. Historical trend analysis is impossible. I would persist shadow results to PostgreSQL alongside scoring results, with a TTL-based cleanup.
06
No automated retraining on drift alert
When PSI crosses 0.25, the system alerts but does not act. A human must investigate and decide whether to retrain. I would add an automated retraining trigger that runs the full training pipeline, evaluates the retrained model in shadow mode, and promotes only if it passes both performance and fairness gates.
07
Adverse action codes capped at 4
ECOA requires "specific reasons" but does not mandate exactly 4. Some declines have 6 or 7 meaningful risk factors. I would make the count configurable (default 4, max 8) and let the consumer decide how many reasons to surface.
08
Redis caching not fully implemented
Redis is configured with 3600s TTL, but actual feature caching is minimal. Repeated scoring of the same borrower recomputes all 44 features from scratch. I would cache the full feature vector keyed by borrower_id with cache invalidation on profile update events.
