Project

Graph Recommendation Engine

Engineering Brief
Problem Recommend products to anonymous users with no purchase history. Only signal: a short browsing session of 5-6 items. Approach Build a co-occurrence graph of 82K items from 172K sessions. Compare GraphSAGE, GAT, and Graph Transformer architectures with Laplacian positional encodings. Result 38.28% Recall@10 (2.6x over GraphSAGE baseline). Removing FFN layers gave 88x training speedup with only 3% accuracy loss. Why it matters Most e-commerce users never log in. Graph-based recommendations work without user profiles, scale to millions of items, and serve in real time via ONNX. Stack PyTorch, PyTorch Geometric, FAISS, FastAPI, ONNX, GCP Vertex AI, MLflow
Chapter 1. The problem

You run an e-commerce site. A user lands on your page. They browse a few products: a wireless keyboard, a mouse, a monitor stand. They have not logged in. They have no account. You have no purchase history, no profile, no preferences. All you know is what they have looked at in the last five minutes.

Can you recommend what they should look at next?

Traditional recommendation systems (like Netflix or Spotify) rely on user profiles. They know your history: what you watched, what you liked, what you skipped. They compare you to similar users and recommend accordingly. But on most e-commerce sites, the majority of users never log in. They are anonymous. Profile-based systems are useless here.

What you need is a system that can recommend products based purely on the current browsing session. A short sequence of 5-6 items. No history. No identity. Just "this person looked at A, B, and C. What should they see next?"

Chapter 2. What does the raw data actually look like?

Before choosing any model, you need to look at the data. The RetailRocket dataset has 2.76 million events: a user viewed an item, added it to cart, or bought it. Each event is a row in a table with a timestamp, a user ID, an item ID, and an event type. That is it. No product descriptions. No categories. No images. Just "user 4829 viewed item 1172 at 3:42 PM."

So the question becomes: where is the signal hiding?

Chapter 3. Where is the signal?

Look at that event log again. There are no product descriptions. No categories. No images. No reviews. No prices. Nothing that tells you what an item is. So where is the recommendation signal hiding?

It is in the co-occurrence patterns. When user 4829 views item 1172, then views item 3390, then views item 7241 in the same sitting, that sequence contains information. Not about the items themselves, but about the relationships between them. If thousands of different users view item 1172 and then view item 3390, there is a connection between those two items. Something about 1172 leads people to 3390.

You do not need to know that item 1172 is a keyboard and item 3390 is a mouse. The behavior tells you they are related. The signal is not in any single row of the table. It is in the pattern across rows. Which items tend to appear together in the same session? How frequently? And if A relates to B and B relates to C, is there an indirect signal from A to C?

This is the key realization. The data is not a table of item features. It is a network of behavioral relationships. Co-occurrence gives you direct connections. Frequency gives you edge weights. Transitivity gives you multi-hop structure. The question now is: what data structure captures all three?

Chapter 4. The obvious approaches (and why they fall short)

Approach 1: Treat it as a table. Build a user-item matrix. Rows are users, columns are items, values are interaction counts. Apply collaborative filtering or matrix factorization. This is textbook. But it assumes you have a user identity to build a row for. Anonymous users do not have a row. And the matrix is extremely sparse: 2.76M events across 235K items means most cells are empty. Collaborative filtering struggles with this level of sparsity.

Approach 2: Treat it as a sequence. Each session is a sequence of item IDs: [1172, 3390, 7241]. Use an RNN or Transformer to predict the next item. This works, but it treats every session as independent. A session [keyboard, mouse] and a session [keyboard, monitor] share the keyboard, but a sequence model learns nothing from that overlap unless both sessions appear in training. It cannot generalize across sessions that share items.

Approach 3: Treat it as a graph. This is the insight. Instead of modeling users or sequences, model the relationships between items. If two items appear in the same session, they are connected. The more sessions they co-appear in, the stronger the connection. Now the keyboard is explicitly linked to mice, monitors, desk mats, and USB hubs. A model that learns on this graph can generalize: it knows that keyboards relate to mice regardless of which specific session the pair appeared in.

Chapter 5. Building the graph

The raw event log is grouped into sessions. If a user is inactive for more than 30 minutes, a new session starts. Sessions shorter than 3 items are dropped (too little signal). This leaves 172,066 sessions across 82,173 unique items.

Then, for every session, each pair of items that appear within a sliding window of 5 positions gets an edge. If item A and item B co-appear in 200 sessions, their edge weight is 200. The result: a graph with 82,173 nodes and 737,716 edges. On average, each item is connected to 18 other items.

Now every product has neighbors. A keyboard is connected to mice, monitors, desk mats, and USB hubs. A winter coat is connected to scarves, gloves, and boots. The graph captures what people actually browse together, not what a product catalog says is related.

Chapter 6. What does this graph actually look like?

Numbers alone do not tell the story. 82,173 nodes and 737,716 edges. But what does that mean in practice? What is the shape of this graph, and what does that shape tell you about how to model it?

Density. Each item is connected to about 18 other items on average. That is sparse. In a fully connected graph of 82K items, each node would have 82,172 edges. We have 18. This means most items are not directly related to most other items. The graph has clear structure, not noise.

Edge weight distribution. The median edge weight is 1. Most pairs of items co-occur in only a single session. The mean is 2.4. But the maximum is 1,424. This is a power law: a small number of item pairs are very strongly related (viewed together by thousands of users), while the vast majority are weakly related (co-occurred once or twice). The model needs to learn that a co-occurrence of 1,424 is a fundamentally different signal than a co-occurrence of 1.

Session structure. The average session has 5.6 items, but only 3.7 unique items (people revisit items). The median session is 4 items long and lasts about 6 minutes. Some sessions are as long as 417 items. This tells you: sessions are short. The model must extract signal from very few items.

What this means for model depth. With an average degree of 18, a 2-layer GNN lets each item "see" about 18 + 18×18 = 342 other items. That covers a meaningful neighborhood without over-smoothing (a known problem where too many layers cause all embeddings to converge to the same values). Deeper is not better here. 2 layers is the sweet spot.

Chapter 7. How a graph neural network learns

A graph neural network (GNN) gives every item a set of numbers (an embedding) that captures its meaning and its position in the graph. But unlike a simple lookup table, these numbers are not fixed. Each item updates its embedding by looking at its neighbors.

Think of it like a rumor network. Each person starts with their own opinion. Then they talk to their friends and update their opinion based on what they hear. After one round, each person’s opinion reflects their immediate circle. After two rounds, it reflects friends-of-friends. The more rounds (layers), the more of the graph each node "sees."

Message passing in a GNN
Item: Wireless Keyboard
Layer 1: looks at its direct neighbors (mouse, monitor, USB hub).
Its embedding now reflects "things people buy with keyboards."
Layer 2: its neighbors have already absorbed THEIR neighbors.
So the keyboard now indirectly knows about mousepads, cable organizers, desk lamps.
Two hops of context.
This project uses 2 layers. With an average degree of 18, each item "sees" roughly 18 + 18×18 = 342 items after 2 layers.

The question is: how should each item combine information from its neighbors? This is where the three model architectures differ.

Chapter 8. Three approaches, compared

GraphSAGE is the simplest approach. Each item takes the average of all its neighbors’ embeddings and combines it with its own. Simple, fast, but it treats all neighbors equally. A keyboard connected to a mouse (strong signal) gets the same weight as a keyboard connected to a random novelty item someone browsed once (weak signal).

GAT (Graph Attention Network) adds attention. Instead of averaging all neighbors equally, it learns which neighbors matter more. The keyboard pays more attention to the mouse (frequently co-purchased) and less to the novelty item (rarely co-purchased). This is a meaningful improvement: 20.10% Recall vs 14.79% for GraphSAGE.

Graph Transformer takes this further. In a standard GAT, each item only attends to its direct neighbors. A Graph Transformer allows each item to attend to all items in its computation subgraph, with multi-head attention (the same mechanism that powers ChatGPT and other language models). This gives it a much richer view of the graph.

Chapter 9. The position problem

There is a subtle problem with graph neural networks. Two items can have identical neighborhoods but be in completely different parts of the graph. Imagine two keyboards: one connected to gaming mice, RGB lighting, and mechanical switches. The other connected to office mice, ergonomic wrist rests, and standing desks. Both are "keyboards with 18 neighbors." Without positional information, a GNN would give them the same embedding.

In language models, this is solved by positional encoding: "this word is at position 5 in the sentence." But graphs don’t have a natural ordering. There is no "position 5."

The solution is Laplacian positional encoding. You compute the eigenvectors of the graph’s Laplacian matrix (a matrix that captures the structure of connections). Each eigenvector acts like a "frequency" on the graph. Low-frequency eigenvectors capture large-scale community structure (gaming gear vs office gear). High-frequency eigenvectors capture fine-grained local differences. Each item gets 16 of these values as a positional fingerprint.

The practical effect: items that are close in the graph get similar positional encodings. Items far apart get different ones. The two keyboards are now distinguishable.

Why positional encoding matters
Without positional encoding
Gaming keyboard and office keyboard have identical local neighborhoods (18 neighbors each). The model sees them as the same item. It cannot distinguish them.
With Laplacian PE (k=16)
Each item gets a 16-number fingerprint based on its global position in the graph. The gaming keyboard is in the "gaming cluster." The office keyboard is in the "office cluster." Now the model can distinguish them.
Chapter 10. The engineering insight that changed everything

The standard Graph Transformer architecture includes feed-forward network (FFN) layers after each attention layer. These are dense layers that expand the embedding dimension by 4x and then compress it back. In language models, FFN layers store factual knowledge ("Paris is the capital of France"). They are essential.

But graph recommendations are different. There are no "facts" to memorize. The model only needs to learn relationships between items. The FFN layers add massive compute cost with almost no benefit.

Removing the FFN layers produced a surprising result:

The model without FFN layers is not just cheaper. It is more accurate. The FFN layers were adding noise, not signal. This is a case where understanding the domain (graph relationships, not factual knowledge) led to an architectural decision that standard practice would never suggest.

Chapter 11. The data

The model is trained on RetailRocket, a public e-commerce dataset with 2.76 million events (views, add-to-carts, purchases) over 4.5 months. Events are sessionized with a 30-minute inactivity gap. Sessions shorter than 3 items are dropped. This leaves 172,066 sessions across 82,173 unique items.

The data is split temporally: 70% training, 15% validation, 15% test, with a 2-day blackout period between each split. The blackout prevents information leakage. Without it, a session that spans the split boundary could leak future information into training.

Chapter 12. How training works

The model learns by predicting what comes next in a session. Given items [A, B, C], predict D. The training signal is a listwise loss: score the correct next item higher than 5 randomly sampled negative items. This is cross-entropy ranking, the same loss used in learning-to-rank systems.

To represent a session, the model takes the embeddings of all items in the session and averages them (mean pooling). This "session embedding" is compared to candidate item embeddings via cosine similarity. The candidate with the highest similarity is the recommendation.

Chapter 14. How it serves in production

Once trained, the item embeddings are frozen. The model is exported to ONNX for fast inference. A FastAPI service accepts a list of item IDs (the current session), looks up their embeddings, averages them into a session vector, and finds the nearest items via cosine similarity. Session Input

The client sends a list of item IDs representing the user’s current browsing session. No user identity needed. No login required. Just the items they looked at.

Embedding Lookup

Each item ID maps to a 256-dimensional vector that was learned during training. These embeddings are frozen at serving time. They encode both the item’s features and its position in the product graph.

82,173 items × 256 dims = ~80MB embedding table
Session Pooling

All item embeddings in the session are averaged into a single 256-dim "session vector." This captures the overall intent of the browsing session. Mean pooling is simple but effective because the GNN has already encoded relational information into each item’s embedding.

Candidate Ranking

The session vector is compared to all 82K item embeddings via cosine similarity (dot product of normalized vectors). The top-K highest scoring items become the recommendations. Items already in the session are excluded.

FastAPI Service

Production API with POST /recommend (single session) and POST /recommend/batch (multiple sessions). Model exported to ONNX for optimized inference. Health check at /health returns model info and item count.

Trained on GCP Vertex AI with NVIDIA L4 GPU (24GB VRAM). Experiments tracked with MLflow.
