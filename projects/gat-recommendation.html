Project

Session-Based Recommendations with Graph Neural Networks

Predicting what anonymous shoppers buy next, on a $300 budget
Engineering Brief
Problem Anonymous e-commerce visitors with no purchase history. Only signal: the last 3 to 10 items they browsed. Traditional recommenders (collaborative filtering) cannot work here. Approach Build a co-occurrence graph (82K items, 738K edges) from 172K sessions. Compare GraphSAGE, GAT, and Graph Transformer architectures. Temporal splits with blackout periods to prevent data leakage. Laplacian positional encoding to solve structural equivalence. Result 38.28% Recall@10 (2.6x over baseline). Removed FFN layers from the Graph Transformer: 88x faster training, 90x cheaper ($21 vs $1,880), and 1.6% better accuracy. The cheaper model was the better model. Stack PyTorch, PyTorch Geometric, DVC, FastAPI, ONNX Runtime, GCP Vertex AI, Terraform, Docker, MLflow, Prometheus
Chapter 1. The cold-start problem

Most e-commerce visitors never log in. No account, no purchase history, no profile. The only signal is a short browsing session: the last 3 to 10 items they looked at. Traditional recommendation systems like collaborative filtering need user history to work. They are useless for anonymous visitors.

I needed a system that could recommend the next item based purely on a short browsing session. No user identity. No metadata about the products. Just "this person looked at A, B, and C, what should they see next?" across a catalogue of 82,000 items.

The constraint that shaped everything: I had $300 in GCP credits, multiple projects to deliver, and the standard Graph Transformer architecture wanted $1,880 just for training. So the question became: can I make it dramatically cheaper and still get the accuracy?

17,266 items had no co-occurrence signal and are excluded. The graph captures what people actually browse together.

The insight is that the data is not a table of user preferences. It is a network of item relationships. If two items appear in the same session, they are connected. The more sessions they co-appear in, the stronger the edge. A keyboard is connected to mice, monitors, and desk mats because that is what people browse together. No product metadata needed.

The graph has 82,173 nodes and 737,716 edges. Average degree of 18 (each item connects to about 18 others). Edge weights follow a power law: median weight of 1, max of 1,424. The model needs to treat a co-occurrence of 1,424 very differently from a co-occurrence of 1.

Chapter 3. Three decisions that shaped the architecture
01
Temporal splits with blackout periods
The data is split 70/15/15 by time, not randomly. A 2-day blackout period between each split prevents information leakage. Without the blackout, a session spanning the split boundary could leak future browsing behavior into training data. This is a detail that most academic implementations skip, but it is the difference between honest and dishonest evaluation.
02
Laplacian positional encoding
Two items can have identical neighborhoods but sit in completely different parts of the graph. A gaming keyboard connected to RGB mice and a office keyboard connected to ergonomic wrist rests both have 18 neighbors. Without positional information, the GNN treats them identically. Laplacian PE computes 16 eigenvectors of the graph's Laplacian matrix, giving each item a structural fingerprint. Low-frequency eigenvectors capture community structure (gaming vs office clusters). High-frequency ones capture local differences.
03
Removed FFN layers (the 88x speedup)
Standard Graph Transformers include feed-forward network layers after each attention layer. In language models, FFN layers store factual knowledge. But graph recommendations have no "facts" to memorize, only relationships. I removed the FFN layers entirely. The result: 88x faster training, 90x cheaper, and 1.6% better accuracy. The FFN layers were adding parameters that caused slight overfitting (112K vs 46K params). Removing them acted as implicit regularization.

Recall@10 means: out of 82,000 possible items, the correct next item appeared in my top 10 recommendations 38% of the time. The optimized model is not just cheaper. It is more accurate. The smaller parameter count (46K vs 112K) acts as a regularizer, preventing the overfitting that the full architecture was prone to.

The Graph Transformer with FFN was estimated at $1,880 for training. I could not afford to run it. The optimized version trained for $21. The budget constraint forced an architectural decision that turned out to be the better engineering choice.

2 GNN layers is the sweet spot. Avg degree 18, so 2 hops covers ~324 items. 3+ layers cause over-smoothing.

Each item starts with a learned 256-dim embedding plus a Laplacian positional encoding (16 eigenvectors projected to 256 dims). Two layers of TransformerConv with multi-head attention let each item update its embedding based on its graph neighborhood. At inference, a session's item embeddings are averaged into a single session vector, then ranked against all 82K items by cosine similarity.

Training uses BPR (Bayesian Personalized Ranking) loss: given items [A, B, C] in a session, score the correct next item higher than 5 randomly sampled negatives. AdamW optimizer, learning rate 1e-3, batch size 32. Early stopping with patience of 10 epochs. The model converged at epoch 57 of 67.

Chapter 6. The engineering stack
01
ML Engineering
PyTorch + PyTorch Geometric for model training. DVC pipeline (5 stages: sessionize, split, build_graph, validate, train) for reproducibility. Ablation studies across FFN vs no-FFN, number of layers, negative sampling count, and positional encoding variants.
02
Production Serving
FastAPI with POST /recommend (single session) and POST /recommend/batch. Item embeddings exported to ONNX Runtime for inference. The ONNX model does pure dot-product ranking at serving time, no GNN forward pass needed. Docker images for both training and serving.
03
Cloud Infrastructure
GCP Vertex AI for training on NVIDIA L4 GPU (24GB VRAM). Terraform for infrastructure-as-code: Artifact Registry, GCS buckets with lifecycle rules, service accounts with scoped IAM roles. Cloud Build pipelines for CI/CD. Prometheus for monitoring.
04
Code Quality
pytest test suite, mypy type checking, ruff linting, black formatting. MLflow for experiment tracking. Full C4 architecture documentation (context, containers, components, code-level walkthroughs).
